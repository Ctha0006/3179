Victoria Work-related fatalities

WorkSafe is Victoria's workplace health and safety regulator.
The following visualisation presents confirmed work-related fatalities
from Jan 2018 - Dec 2022 as provided by WorkSafe Victoria.

Total Fatalities by Month and Year

A work-related fatality is defined as any of the following:
1. the death of any person where the death occurs: at a workplace or arising from the conduct of the undertaking of an employer/manager; and where the person was owed a duty under the Occupational Health and Safety Act 2004 by an employer or management
2. the death of a worker, within the meaning of the Workplace Injury Rehabilitation and Compensation Act 2013 (WIRC Act)
3. the death of an employer or self-employed person
which WorkSafe Victoria has been notified.

There were 66 recorded worker fatalities in 2022. This is the lowest of recorded in 3 years. There has generally been no change in the number of recorded fatalities each year apart from an increase of 27 fatalities from 2018 to 2019. The highest number of work-related injury fatalities in a month was 12 fatalities in April 2019 and March of 2021.

In 2022, the average fatalities slightly decreased from a previous 6.75 to 5.5 fatalities per month. 2018 had the smallest average of 4.4. This may be due to missing data or not all fatalities being recorded.

Work-Related Fatality Categories

Lives lost in Victorian workplaces recorded by WorkSafe Victoria previously excluded some work-related deaths that were not attributable to obligations under the Occupational Health and Safety Act 2004, such as those caused by transport accidents or occupational diseases.

From July 2020 this was changed and workers who die on the road, suicides attributable to work, industrial diseases such as silicosis, and workplace deaths resulting from a criminal act, are included.

Traumatic Injury/Events was the biggest category of fatalities at 41%.

The second largest category was Transport Accident (20%).

Asphyxia (death by suffocation), Criminal Offence, and Other categories were the smallest recorded fatalities, totaling 2%. Suicide has gradually decreased over the years.

Fatalities due to Lung Disease was increasing from 2018 to 2021 however has decreased in 2022. Medical Complications and Medical incl Heart/Stroke did not have much change over the years.

Work Industries

Over the years, the majority (17%) of fatalities occurred in Construction (126 fatalities). Transport, postal and warehousing (106 fatalities) accounted for 15% of fatalities. This was followed by Manufacturing (104 fatalities; 14%) and Public Administration and Safety (98 fatalities; 14%).

It is interesting to note that these 4 industries mentioned had fatalities every year from 2018 to 2022, in addition to Agriculture, Forestry and Fishing and Wholesale Trade which had the next highest total fatalities. Their top 4 heirachy over the years varied slightly amongs the 4 majority mentioned.

It is understandable to see more fatalities in such industries in comparison to Information Media and Telecommunications or Rental, Hiring and Real Estate Services which are not physical labour. Mining did not have any recorded fatalities until 2021.

Fatalities by Age Group and Gender

From 2018 - 2022, 92% of workers killed were Male. The data shows from the sum of fatalities from 2018-2022, that workers aged 55 to 64 years had significantly higher number of fatalities at 83, followed by workers aged 45 to 54 years at 61 fatalities. The sum was consistent with male fatality trends however it is noticed that the female fatalities did not have significantly high numbers. The highest number of fatalities for Females was 7 people for workers aged 35 to 44.

Fatalities by Local Government Areas (LGA)

of 4.95 (6 Total Fatalities). The City of Greater Dandenong has the highest number of total fatalities of 20 though their taio per 10,000 population was low at 1.54. Melbourne City had the
